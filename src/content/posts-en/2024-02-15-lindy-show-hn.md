Lindy's development team posted to Hacker News's "Show HN" section, introducing the platform broadly to the developer community as a way to build AI agents with no code. About a year after its public launch, the product entered a phase of expanding recognition beyond consumers to reach engineers as well.

## Details

- **Post content**: Introduced a system for assembling an agent ("Lindy") simply by providing a prompt, choosing from over 3,000 API connectors, and adding event-based triggers
- **Team functionality**: Already featured the ability to connect multiple Lindys to work together as a team, demonstrating a design philosophy that went beyond single agents
- **Marketplace concept**: Revealed plans for a "Lindy Store" where users could share and install agents created by others, with monetization features also planned for the future
- **Real-world examples**: The poster shared a wide range of personal use cases for Lindy, including meeting transcription, schedule management, flashcard generation, content summarization, mood tracking, customer support automation, a recruiting assistant, and data analysis

## What happened next

Following this Hacker News exposure, Lindy steadily expanded its feature set. That July, the company shipped "Lindy 2.0," a major upgrade to workflow building that evolved the platform into a foundation capable of handling more complex automations.
